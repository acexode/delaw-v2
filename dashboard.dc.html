Can the State compulsorily acquire land for a private commercial purpose?
5 authorities · Nigeria · Supreme Court, Court of Appeal
{{ sparkIcon }}AI AnswerGROUNDED
No — not for a purely private commercial purpose. Under s.28 Land Use Act and s.44(1) of the 1999 Constitution, acquisition is lawful only for an overriding public interest with prompt, adequate compensation. The Supreme Court held acquisition for a private benefit to be ultra vires.

Bello v. Attorney-General of Oyo State

(1986) 5 NWLR (Pt. 45) 828, SC
Supreme Court of Nigeria · 15 December 1986 · Appeal No. SC.96/1985
Coram: Obaseki, Eso, Nnamani, Uwais & Oputa, JJSC

Facts

The appellant's land at Bodija, Ibadan, was compulsorily acquired by the Oyo State Government under the Public Lands Acquisition Law. It later emerged that the land was allocated to a private individual. The appellant challenged the acquisition as a violation of his right to property.

Issue

Whether a compulsory acquisition effected for the benefit of a private person, rather than for an overriding public purpose, is valid in law.

Holding

The Supreme Court held that compulsory acquisition of land must serve a genuine public purpose. An acquisition that confers a benefit on a private individual is ultra vires the acquiring authority and is liable to be set aside; the payment of compensation cannot validate an otherwise unlawful acquisition.

{{ highlightIcon }}Your note: Lead authority for LCCI v. AG Lagos — quote in para 3.2.

Ratio decidendi

The power of compulsory acquisition is a public power to be exercised only for public purposes; it cannot be used as a conduit to transfer one citizen's property to another.

What are the requirements for a valid arbitration agreement under the Arbitration & Mediation Act 2023? Does it need to be signed by both parties?
{{ sparkIcon }}
DeLaw AIGROUNDEDNIGERIA

Under the Arbitration & Mediation Act 2023, an arbitration agreement is valid if it meets three requirements:

  1. In writing — s.2(3) treats it as written if its content is recorded in any form, including electronic communication.
  2. Clear intention to arbitrate — the parties must have agreed to submit disputes to arbitration.
  3. A defined legal relationship — whether contractual or not.

On signatures: no, signatures by both parties are not strictly required. An exchange of correspondence in which the agreement is asserted and not denied suffices under s.2(4).

Arbitration & Mediation Act 2023, ss.2–3M.V. Lupex v. N.O.C. (2003) 15 NWLR 469
